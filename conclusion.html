In conclusion, it is evident that hotspots and local densification of ethnic groups do exist.
However, patterns are beginning to change as a result of the growth of particular ethnic
groups in new areas. This is increasingly occuring due to older immigrants moving out
of hotspots (e.g. Indian and Lebanese) or new immigrants moving into new growth suburbs
(e.g. Chinese immigrants moving into city suburbs). Some ancestries (predominantly English
speaking) do not have any high density clusters and instead tend to be scattered around
Sydney due to their ability to adapt to any given environment.

Is it good to change the patterns observed?

This is a debatable question as there are both sides to the arguement. However, it is best to
keep local hotspots of different ethnic groups as it currently is. The reason for this is that
humans have an instinctive desire to be with like-minded people and those who share the
same beliefs and morals. For this reason, staying with those with the same background can
create a greater sense of belonging especially within a foreign country. This is predominately
the case for new and first generation immigrants. However, on the other side, by encouraging
new immigrants to be in a diverse community it creates an opportunity for them to interact
with different people which will allow them to adapt eaiser than those who move later on.

Recent attempts in the form of new residential developments to reduce clustering of ethnic
groups have proven to be successful. Areas such as The Ponds, Kellyville and Schofields
are now home to a diverse range of people. Perhaps this is the best solution to keep a "balance"
in the spread of different ethnic groups.
